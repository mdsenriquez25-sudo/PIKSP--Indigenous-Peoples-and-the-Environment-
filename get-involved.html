Issues and Problems on Biodiversity Conservation of Indigenous Lands in the Philippines

• Overexploitation. Overexploitation of preferred tree species for construction, handicrafts, and other uses, driven by growing community demand, leads to a decline in their population.

• Land use alteration. Clearing forests for agriculture, especially through slash-and-burn practices before the rainy season, is a serious problem in some Indigenous communities.

• Forest fire. Forest fires, often unintentionally caused by burning agricultural clearings, harm most plant and animal species and commonly occur in grasslands or areas under natural succession during summer or prolonged drought.

•Limited knowledge of the global value of biodiversity. Many provincial community members have limited awareness of biodiversity’s broader environmental benefits—such as environmental protection, carbon sequestration, and climate change mitigation—despite some limited information dissemination efforts.

• Limited capacity of barangay government units. Though national environmental laws and even local government ordinances for biodiversity protection are existing, the barangay government units cannot implement such laws due to their limited financial, physical, and manpower resources

Lack of sustainable livelihood and alternative sources of income. Due to the absence of sustainable livelihood and a source of income, people tend to look to the available natural resources as a source of their subsistence and income.

Contemporary Challenges on Land Use and Management in Philippine Indigenous Lands

• Issues on the ancestral domain; loss of land property. Although ancestral domains were generally easy to identify and delineate based on the locations of ethnic communities, boundary issues and disputes still exist between some provinces.

• Land titling. This is the act of formal government to classify lands according to their different uses as the watershed, forest or timberland, parks and wildlife reservations, alienable and disposable lands for agriculture, residential and commercial use.

• Threat to the watershed. Although dams were built to irrigate fields, generate electricity, and provide income for provinces, there is a need to review profit-sharing schemes to ensure fair distribution and better protection of the watershed ecosystem.

• Boundary disputes. Ongoing boundary disputes between Indigenous communities, particularly in neighboring provinces, need to be resolved promptly as they hinder progress.
