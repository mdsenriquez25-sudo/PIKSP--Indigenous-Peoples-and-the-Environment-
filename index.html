Indigenous Peoples And The Environment

ENVIRONMENTAL CONSERVATION AND SUSTAINABILITY PRACTICES OF INDIGENOUS PEOPLES

Indigenous peoples manage a large part of the world’s land, but climate change still threatens their livelihoods. By 2100, Philip Alston warns that climate change could create “climate apartheid,” where the rich escape the worst effects while the poor face hunger, conflict, or migration.

Who are we talking about when we refer to indigenous peoples; and what do we mean when we say that land is traditionally owned, managed, or occupied?

There is no single definition of indigenous peoples because they are diverse. According to the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, they are communities with historical connections to their lands before colonization, strong ties to natural resources, and distinct cultures, languages, and social systems. They maintain traditional knowledge based on long interaction with nature, viewing it as sacred and using this knowledge to protect environmental balance and sustain resources like water, food, soil, shelter, and medicine.

How are indigenous people affected by changes in climate, biodiversity, and ecosystems?

Indigenous peoples are highly affected by environmental degradation and climate change because their lives depend on their land and natural resources. Activities like industrial projects, deforestation, and even some climate solutions such as biofuel plantations can harm their territories. However, when their land and resource rights are protected, both their communities and the environment can flourish.

What role do indigenous people play in developing, managing, and protecting natural space and ecosystem?

Indigenous peoples play an important role in creating solutions for protecting ecosystems. Their traditional knowledge helps in environmental management, sustainable food production, and climate change mitigation. Organizations like United Nations Environment Programme work with the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues to promote traditional knowledge and support ecosystem restoration and sustainability.

On a policy level, how can we ensure that indigenous people are included in the decision- making and management of ecosystem?

The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples requires that indigenous peoples give free, prior, and informed consent for decisions affecting their rights and lands. Groups like the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues and the Expert Mechanism on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples help represent their concerns. Meanwhile, the United Nations Environment Programme supports the protection of environmental defenders and works with partners such as the Interfaith Rainforest Initiative to protect indigenous rights, traditional knowledge, and nature.
